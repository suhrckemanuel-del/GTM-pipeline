### 2. Adaptive ML · Alessandro Cappelli · alessandro@adaptive-ml.com
**Subject:** Adaptive ML vertical focus — one quarter experiment

Hi Alessandro,

"Applied ML" is a hard starting point for a buyer who doesn't already know what problem they're solving. You're asking them to imagine the product in their context before you've given them a reason to care.

The pattern that tends to work in crowded ML tooling: pick one vertical, commit to it publicly for a quarter, and build everything around it. Not just the landing page. The outbound hook, the customer examples on the site, the objection FAQ, the KPI you tie to in the close. When all of that rhymes, referrals get easier because customers can say "we use Adaptive ML for X" without having to explain what ML is.

The cost is saying no to hero copy that tries to cover everything. Which is a real tradeoff. But right now, horizontal positioning means every prospect writes their own product story in their head, which slows down discovery and makes it hard to build sales playbooks.

Happy to sketch out what that vertical kit could look like for one specific use case.

### 3. Cognee · Boris Arzentar · boris@cognee.ai
**Subject:** Cognee dev vs enterprise — two front doors test

Hi Boris,

OSS growth and enterprise revenue tend to need different entry points. Developers who find Cognee through GitHub are evaluating with totally different criteria than security and platform buyers who need to justify a procurement decision internally. If both groups land on the same page with the same "memory layer" narrative, the enterprise deals stall on governance and data residency questions that your OSS users never asked.

The piece that usually moves the needle most is an evaluation kit, something that tells buyers what to test in two weeks, what logs tell you retrieval quality is working, and what the failure modes actually look like. Buyers who don't have that framework tend to build their own, which slows everything down and introduces comparison criteria you didn't set.

Enterprise teams evaluating model providers ask a different first question than developers do. Developers want to know if the model is good. **Subject:** n8n commercial boundary — quick thought

Hi Erla,

The comparison n8n buyers are running in their heads is usually three-way: you against Zapier/Make, and you against whatever internal engineering time would cost. That third comparison is where the commercial boundary story matters most, because "we could just build it" is a real option for a lot of your best-fit customers.

A fuzzy boundary makes procurement cycles longer. If it's not clear who n8n cloud is for vs self-hosted, or what enterprise unlocks beyond hosting, buyers have to figure that out in discovery rather than showing up already convinced.

A simple decision guide ("choose cloud if..., choose self-host if...") cuts a lot of that evaluation back-and-forth. The teams who've built something like this usually see conversion improve from the active free user base, because the path to paid stops requiring a sales call to understand.

### 6. Lovable · Henrik Westerlund · henrik@lovable.dev
**Subject:** Lovable upgrade triggers — cohort test idea

Hi Henrik,

PLG products with strong communities tend to hit a specific ceiling: usage is high, but upgrade conversion is hard to optimize because indie hackers, agencies, and small teams using the product for client work all look the same in the funnel. One-size upgrade prompts end up tuned for nobody in particular.

The test I'd run: separate "solo builder shipping their own thing" from "team shipping to clients" as distinct upgrade paths, each with a different measurable trigger. For solo builders it's probably about project count or publish workflows. For teams and agencies it's about collaboration controls and client-facing features. A cohort-based message to power users in each group, rather than a single upgrade modal, usually lifts conversion and helps you figure out which cohort a sales-assist motion should focus on vs which should stay self-serve.

Curious if you're already running experiments on this, or if the funnel currently treats all upgraders the same.

## On attachments
Don't attach a CV on first contact. It signals job applicant mode, which undercuts the peer-to-peer tone of the rest of the email.

What to do instead:
- Add your LinkedIn URL in your email signature
- The "happy to sketch something out" line in the email is the hook — if they reply, that's when you offer more
- If you have a 1-2 page work sample (an experiment brief, a positioning teardown, anything from this project), you can attach one relevant thing to emails where it fits naturally — but only if it's clean and specific, not a general portfolio PDF

## On being clear about startup interest
Yes, be clear — but the framing matters. "I'm looking at early GTM roles at B2B startups in Europe right now" reads as someone with options who chose to reach out. "I'm a student looking for an internship" reads as an applicant. The emails use the first framing. Each company gets a slightly different version so it feels personal and not templated.
