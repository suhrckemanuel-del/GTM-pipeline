Hi Brijesh,

Being on Sifted's Rising 100 in AI infrastructure right now means you're already in the serious comparison set. That's the hard part done.

Pair that with a light TCO sketch: three inputs, one scenario, not a full calculator. It turns "we'll stay on cloud for now" into a specific comparison they can run against their actual setup.

One public benchmark anchored to a named workload pattern would tie it together.

Happy to sketch what that looks like for one specific workload.

> Why this insight: AI compute looks like a commodity from the outside (price per GPU-hour). The escape isn't to compete on price — it's to make the buyer's workload problem specific before price comes up. Workload anchoring does that. ### 3. Octonomy · [Name — research needed] · [email — research needed]
**Subject:** Octonomy pilot structure — one thing that clears the stall

Hi [Name],

Going after complex enterprise automation with agents on a $20M seed is a high-conviction bet. The market timing is real.

The thing that tends to stall pilots for products like this: the business case stays implicit. Champions believe in it. Procurement, IT, and security each need something different before they can align on scope. "Intelligent automation" doesn't give them a shared frame to work from.

Worth building: three repeatable use-case blueprints — one per industry or function — each with a specific trigger, agent steps, human review gates, and 90-day KPIs. Publish one redacted pilot timeline, week by week, so champions can paste it into internal memos without writing it from scratch.

That shifts each pilot from "let's see what happens" to a defined milestone everyone already agreed to before day one.

Happy to sketch what those blueprints could include for one specific vertical.

I'm looking at early GTM roles in B2B AI in Europe. Octonomy is on my list.

Manuel

Pilots start, drag on for 4-6 months, and don't convert because the success criteria were never explicit enough for procurement to approve the next phase. ### 4. Lakera AI · Paul Shin · paul.shin@lakera.ai
**Subject:** Lakera AI — one addition for the dual-thread motion

Hi Paul,

Lakera has one of the cleaner positioning stories in LLM security right now. The category is real, the urgency is real, and you have both a developer surface and an enterprise narrative. That combination is hard to build.

The thing worth sharpening: when developer-facing and security-facing content share the same page, each buyer sees half a product. Engineers want SDKs, CI hooks, and a fast eval path. Security and compliance teams want policy controls, dashboards, and artifacts they can put in a risk review. Neither is wrong. They're just different enough that blending them leaves both groups with unanswered questions at exactly the moment they need to say yes.

One specific add: a short "bring your CISO" page — what you log, what you block, what exports for audits, in plain language. Developer path stays fast and technical. Security path gets its own frame. The deal becomes two conversations running in parallel instead of one.

That turns "cool demo, unclear enterprise fit" into a dual-thread close.

Happy to sketch what that structure looks like.

I'm looking at early GTM roles at B2B startups in Europe. Lakera is on my shortlist.

Manuel

---

> **Side note — Lakera AI:** They provide security guardrails for LLM applications — blocking prompt injection, data leakage, and model abuse. Think firewall for AI systems. Switzerland-based, Series A €18.2M.
>
> Why this insight: LLM security has a genuinely unusual dual buyer. Engineers want fast SDK integration. CISOs need governance evidence for their risk reviews and audit requirements. Most companies try to serve both with the same page and serve neither well. The "bring your CISO" document is a concrete, low-effort thing that changes the deal structure — instead of one engineer explaining security to their CISO, you give them a two-pager to hand over. It accelerates a deal stage that usually just sits.
>
> Why strength-first works here: Lakera actually has both developer tooling and enterprise features — they've done the hard product work. The insight is about surfacing what already exists to the right buyer more efficiently, not asking them to build something new.

### 5. Kestra · David Khan · dkhan@kestra.io
**Subject:** Kestra community to commercial — one bridge to build

Hi David,

Kestra's GitHub traction in workflow orchestration is real. Getting engineers to self-select into an open source tool and keep using it is harder than most people assume. The community you've built is a commercial asset.

The gap that tends to open next: engineering teams use it, love it, and then stall when someone asks "what does the paid tier give us?" if the answer requires a discovery call to figure out. Community-to-commercial conversion in open source orchestration turns on two things: a specific moment when teams cross a clear threshold — pipelines, users, audit requirements — and a short champion-ready upgrade story that an engineer can share with their manager without scheduling a sales call.

One thing worth building: a concise "when to upgrade" guide anchored to two or three named triggers, with a clear one-paragraph answer per trigger in engineering language — not a feature comparison table. Pair it with one case study from a team of similar size: what they ran, when they moved to the paid tier, what actually changed.

That bridges the gap between community adoption and first commercial conversation.

Happy to think through what those triggers look like.

I'm looking at GTM roles at developer tools companies in Europe. Kestra is one I'd want to be part of.

Manuel

---

> **Side note — Kestra:** They build an open source workflow orchestration and scheduling platform — like Apache Airflow but more modern and developer-friendly. Used for data pipelines, event-driven automation, infrastructure workflows. Series A-stage, France-based.
>
> Why this insight: open source to enterprise commercial conversion is one of the hardest GTM problems in software. Usage grows, billing doesn't follow, because the commercial tier is either under-explained or requires a conversation to understand. "Upgrade when you need X" sounds obvious but almost no company builds the actual document — in engineering language, for engineers — that explains what X is in practical terms. It's a low-effort add with real conversion impact, and most teams only build it after the problem is obvious in their data.
>
> Why strength-first works here: they've built real GitHub community — the insight is about monetizing what already exists, not questioning the product direction.

**Subject:** OpenFX — one thing for the next corridor

Hi [Name],

A $94M Series A in March is a strong signal — the volume growth story is clearly working in your current markets.

When that growth pushes into new corridors, the pitch changes in a specific way. FX and liquidity buyers in new markets don't ask if the platform is good first. They ask whether you've done this here before. Proof from your current markets doesn't transfer automatically because the questions are corridor-specific: local rails, settlement timelines, compliance for that region.

One thing that tends to work in these expansions: ship corridor-specific content before the sales push, not after. Route, use cases, compliance notes, what "good" latency looks like for that corridor, and one onboarding narrative — steps, testing windows, support. Pair with a narrow outbound list of teams already shipping volume in that market. They convert fastest because the evaluation cycle is short.

It turns "we'll wait for someone with local experience" into a specific comparison you already control.

Happy to think through what that looks like for whichever corridor you're prioritizing next.

I'm looking at early GTM roles at fintech companies in Europe. OpenFX is on my radar.

Manuel

> **Side note — OpenFX:** They provide programmatic access to FX markets and liquidity — APIs for financial institutions and fintechs moving money internationally. US-based, $94M Series A closed March 2026, expanding geographically (Southeast Asia cited in pipeline notes).
>
> Why this insight: international expansion in fintech is notoriously hard to execute from a GTM angle. The product works, but new market buyers have a trust deficit that global proof doesn't fill — they want evidence you've done *this corridor* before, not just that you're good at moving money in general. Corridor-specific content is the standard answer, but almost no company builds it proactively. They build it reactively after deals stall. Getting ahead of it signals local commitment and compresses the evaluation cycle.
